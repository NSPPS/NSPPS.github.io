Poster Resources



Top Tips:

  • Keep your word count under 800 and design for scientists outside your field.
  • Use an A0 portrait (or smaller) poster, as these fit the poster boards best. (We are able to accommodate a limited number of landscape posters. Please do not submit posters larger than A0 size.)
  • Include images and plots. Combined with short amounts of text, figures allow you to convey your message quickly.
  • Keep your poster title concise and include key words.

Printing

The University Print Services will print A0 from pdf. They will be busy, so make sure you get the poster to them 3+ days in advance. You can pay for your poster using your research grant code if you have one.

Useful links

If you'd like a feel for what academic posters look like, browse the Better Academic Posters blog and flickr poster critique group, (though bear in mind that not all of these are good posters!).

For more guidance on making an effective poster see Colin Purrington's academic poster design page. It includes good and bad examples, which are really useful.

For poster making advice in terms of how to use the program of your choice, the university provides a basic guide to making a poster in microsoft powerpoint but do consider alternative programs to design your poster in. Latex, Adobe Illustrator, Serif and many more are all better poster design tools than powerpoint.

If you get stuck, come and ask for an opinion or some advice on our facebook event page.
